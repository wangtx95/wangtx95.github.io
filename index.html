Cora — 911 接线员实时辅助工具 Cora 是一款为 911 接线员提供屏幕端实时辅助的工具，核心功能包括：实时 SOP 检查清单、基于来电详情自动调整的智能提醒、实时绩效数据采集、可定制界面组件、GIS 驱动的情境感知，以及主管监控仪表盘（支持实时监听）。 aurelian Ava + Cora 联动：当 Ava 检测到紧急情况并转接人工时，Cora 会自动接收来电者信息、地址、事件类型和关键陈述，直接打开对应检查清单，接线员无需重新询问，直接接续通话，节省关键时间。 aurelian 竞争优势 水平语音方案和现有系统供应商缺乏关键能力，而 Aurelian 的大多数客户能够单一采购该产品，这是其产品独特性的强信号。911 中心对可靠性要求极高，需要保持系统稳定运行、消除幻觉和漏检（非紧急线路接入的真实紧急呼叫）。 NEA 小结 一、从问题出发：为什么是 911 中心？ 核心矛盾：911 中心同时面临两个无法自行解决的结构性问题： 人员严重短缺：招人难、留人难，调度员离职率高 来电结构失衡：大量占用线路的是噪音投诉、违规停车这类非紧急事务，而非真正的紧急情况 这两个问题叠加，造成的结果是：真正紧急的来电，反而因为线路被占用而响应变慢。 这是一个行业长期存在、且越来越严重的问题，但此前没有好的技术解决方案。 二、为什么之前的方案失败了？ 云时代的供应商试图把 911 中心"云化"，但面临一个根本障碍：CAD 系统（计算机辅助调度系统）是整个 911 中心的核心基础设施，极难替换。任何需要"替换旧系统"的方案，在这个行业几乎没有销售成功的可能。 Aurelian 的核心设计决策就是：不替换，只叠加。Ava 直接接入现有电话线路和 CAD 系统，做增量而非替换，大幅降低了采购阻力。 三、产品设计的底层逻辑 Ava 的设计逻辑：在人之前拦截 非紧急来电的问题不是"处理太慢"，而是"根本不应该占用调度员"。所以 Ava 的位置是在调度员之前，完全自主地完成整个通话——接听、理解、收集信息、录入系统、跟进。 关键设计取舍： 不做"转接辅助"，而是做"全程自主"——因为只有 74% 自动化才能真正释放调度员，转接辅助只是换了个形式的负担 设置紧急情况实时监测——这是让 911 中心敢于部署 AI 接线的信任前提。如果 AI 可能漏掉真实紧急情况，没有任何 ECC 愿意用 区域化语音转录——911 来电者口音多样、地名特殊，通用语音识别在这个场景会频繁出错，必须定制 Cora 的设计逻辑：在人旁边辅助 Ava 处理不了的来电，必然转给人工。但人工接线员的问题是：在压力下容易遗漏 SOP 步骤、处理罕见事件时缺乏经验。Cora 的位置是不替代判断，而是降低认知负担——实时把正确的 checklist 推到屏幕上。 这个设计逻辑是：AI 不应该在高风险场景下做最终决策，但可以让人做出更好的决策。 四、两款产品为什么要一起卖？ 表面上 Ava 和 Cora 是两个独立产品，但它们形成了一个完整的工作流闭环： 来电进入 ↓ Ava 判断：非紧急 → 全程自动处理 ↓ Ava 判断：紧急 → 实时转接 + 传递所有已收集信息 ↓ Cora 接收信息，自动打开对应 SOP checklist ↓ 调度员接手，无需重新询问，直接继续 这个 handoff 设计有两个价值： 效率：节省的几十秒在紧急场景下可能是生死之差 销售：打包卖比单品卖更难被替换，形成更强的系统依赖 五、商业模式设计逻辑 Aurelian 的预算来源是"空缺调度员席位的人力成本" NEA ——ECC 因为招不到人而空着的岗位，本来就有预算，现在用这笔钱买 AI，ROI 叙事非常清晰，不需要额外申请新预算。 这是一个非常聪明的定价锚点设计：用已经存在的人力缺口来证明采购合理性，绕开了大多数政府/公共安全机构的预算审批阻力。 总结：设计逻辑的核心 设计选择 背后逻辑 不替换 CAD，只集成 降低采购阻力，这个行业"替换"等于死亡 Ava 做全程自主而非辅助 只有完全自动化才真正释放调度员 内置紧急情况监测 建立信任是部署的前提，不是加分项 Cora 做辅助而非决策 Product opportunity 01 · 10 → 100

The deployment process is a product problem in disguise

What looks like an ops bottleneck may actually be a missing product layer

When I looked at how Aurelian deploys today, my first reaction was the same: this is a strong team executing well under real constraints. Sub-six-week deployments in a government context are genuinely impressive. But speed alone is not the most important signal. The more important question is whether later deployments are becoming meaningfully easier, or whether too much of the same implementation burden still has to be recreated each time a new ECC goes live.

That distinction matters because not all deployment work is the same. Some of it is inherently high-touch and should probably remain so: cross-agency alignment, local validation, training, trust-building, and technical work that genuinely requires engineering involvement. But some of it appears different. It looks less like bespoke service work and more like repeated configuration work — the kind of work that often begins in deployment, but eventually belongs in a more repeatable product layer.

My hypothesis is that SOP setup may be one of those seams, at least in part. From the outside, a meaningful share of the variation seems to show up in call-type definitions, question sequences, routing logic, and local escalation paths. Those differences are real, and I would not assume they are trivial. Product observation

The key question is not whether every ECC can be standardized. It is whether a large enough middle of the deployment motion is repetitive, structured, and low-risk enough to become progressively more repeatable. The value is specific: less handcrafted configuration per deployment, more reuse, and a motion that gets faster as it scales — not one that stays flat.

To be clear about what product can and cannot own here: model fine-tuning on local call history, CAD integration, and accent-specific transcription still require engineering involvement. But SOP configuration and redirect routing look much closer to true product surfaces. The most realistic first step is probably not a fully self-serve customer tool. Product opportunity 02 · 100 → 1000

CAD integration: from recurring cost to one-time investment

Product can't negotiate the partnership — but it can build the foundation that makes it tractable

CAD integration strategy — whether to pursue certified partnerships, channel deals, or both — belongs to the founders and BD. But inside that motion, product has a narrower role worth naming. Every time Aurelian integrates with a new CAD system, there is real engineering work involved: data contracts, workflow mapping, edge cases, vendor-specific constraints. That complexity is real. What I cannot see from the outside is whether that work is already being absorbed into a cleaner, more reusable foundation — one where each completed integration lowers the marginal cost of the next — or whether each new customer still triggers meaningful reinvention. I would not assume the latter. But I would want to understand it. That distinction matters because it determines whether CAD coverage compounds into a structural advantage over time, or primarily expands capability without much compounding benefit. If the former is already true, product's role here is mostly to make that legible to the commercial motion. If the latter, there may be an architectural conversation worth having — one that PM should probably be the first to put on the table.

Product opportunity 03 · 1000 → scale

The procurement problem: where product supports sales

The politics of government buying are not a product problem — but some of the proof required to move each buyer is

Buying AI for a 911 center is not a single decision. It runs through multiple stakeholders — ECC director, IT security, budget committee, dispatcher union, county legal — each with different risk thresholds and different veto power. Navigating that process is a CEO and sales problem. But inside it, there is a narrower product role worth naming: not to replace persuasion or founder-led trust, but to reduce how much of the factual proof required in each deal has to be rebuilt from scratch. Some of what these buyers need is political. Some of it is relational. But a meaningful share is concrete, repeated, and answerable — and that is the seam product can own.

If those materials are assembled ad hoc in every deal, the sales motion stays founder-dependent. If they are built into standard, deployment-backed artifacts from the start — an ROI dashboard grounded in live results, a security and compliance packet, an escalation report, an audit trail, eventually dispatcher satisfaction or override rates — then the process becomes more legible and less fragile. Product does not own the conversation. But it can ensure the proof layer exists before the founders have to recreate it in every room.

Product observation

These artifacts are strongest when they are product-derived, deployment-backed, and easy to trust because they are generated from the system itself — not assembled as one-off sales slides. The ROI model for the budget committee. The auditability and escalation logic for legal. The security posture for IT. Over time, even the union conversation may benefit from product-generated proof around workload shift, escalation quality, and dispatcher experience.

Section 04 · Judgment

If I were the founding PM: how I'd think about sequencing

Not a conclusion — a decision framework, and an honest invitation to push back

I want to end with something honest: sequencing these three opportunities is not a decision I can make credibly from the outside. Aurelian may ultimately need all three — a more repeatable deployment layer, a cleaner integration foundation, and a stronger product-derived proof layer for procurement. The real question is not which one sounds best in isolation. It is which one removes the next meaningful constraint on growth without creating more execution drag than the company can absorb right now.

That answer depends on a small set of variables that are only fully visible from the inside. If I were stepping into the first PM seat, I would not start by defending a favorite idea. I would start by figuring out which repeated burden is already concentrated enough, costly enough, and ready enough to become the next system the company builds.

Variable 01

Where is demand already concentrated?

If a large share of the next wave of ECC opportunities sits on platforms Aurelian already supports, then deployment repeatability likely has the fastest near-term payoff. If demand is clustering around one or two CAD ecosystems that are not yet covered, integration leverage may matter more. If deals are repeatedly slowing in stakeholder sign-off, then the procurement proof layer may be the more urgent bottleneck.

Variable 02

What commitments are already tied to near-term revenue?

Founding-stage priorities are rarely chosen on a blank page. If near-term revenue depends on faster deployments, that changes the urgency of the configuration layer. If a specific CAD integration is the gating item for a meaningful deal, that changes the calculus. If the company is already doing founder-heavy work to push deals through procurement, the question is whether a stronger proof layer would reduce that burden soon enough to matter.

Variable 03

Which bet has the highest compounding value?

Not every painful problem is the right first system to build. A repeatable deployment layer can lower implementation burden across many customers. A reusable integration foundation can lower expansion friction across future platforms. A stronger proof layer can shorten stakeholder review in future deals. The right first bet is not just the loudest pain point — it is the one most likely to keep paying back after the first use.

Variable 04

Can the organization absorb this bet right now?

Even the right leverage move can be mistimed. Readiness is not just engineering bandwidth — it is whether the company has the foundation, ownership, and operating room to turn one of these burdens into a real system without destabilizing work already tied to current customers and revenue.

Month 1

Shadow every active sales call, deployment standup, and customer check-in. No opinions yet — just mapping where effort is being recreated too often and where deals are slowing down. By end of month one, I want a clear picture of pipeline composition, current CAD coverage gaps, and where founders are spending time that a product artifact could replace.

Month 2

Translate that into a sequencing recommendation: which of the three opportunities is the binding constraint right now, whether they can run in parallel given current engineering bandwidth, and what the first concrete deliverable should be. Not a roadmap — a specific first bet with explicit success criteria.

Month 3

Have something in use. Whether that is a configuration template the implementation team is already pulling from, a cleaner integration pattern reducing friction on the next similar CAD deployment, or a first version of the ROI dashboard removing one founder-hour from a live procurement conversation. The measure is not whether it is built. It is whether someone on the team is already using it.

The trade-off either way is the same: prioritizing one means something else waits, and whatever deal, customer, or internal burden sits behind what gets deprioritized becomes a cost the team is knowingly accepting. That cost should be explicit, tracked, and revisited — not forgotten because a decision was made.

These are my starting observations. I'd genuinely like to know where this matches how you're thinking about it — and where it doesn't. The places we disagree are probably the most interesting ones to talk through.
