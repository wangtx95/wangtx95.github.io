I spent time researching Aurelian before writing this, and what stood out to me is that the product appears to have already cleared a difficult early threshold: it is not just technically interesting, but operationally credible. The combination of meaningful automation, relatively fast deployments, and live adoption across multiple states suggests the team has done something unusually hard for public safety software: built an AI product that can function in a high-stakes, low-trust environment and still get government customers live. That is not common, and it should not be underestimated.

From the outside, the question I kept coming back to is not whether the product works. It is whether the current product form is built for the next stage of scale. Proven viability is one thing; scalability is another. In many companies, that transition is where a founding PM can be especially useful: not by redefining the vision, but by helping the team decide what should remain high-touch, what should become repeatable, and where the next bottleneck is most likely to emerge.

The three observations below are my attempt to frame that transition through a product lens. They are not conclusions. They are hypotheses about where product may have the most leverage, where it should stay in its lane, and which scaling constraints seem most likely to matter next. They are also the places where I would be most interested to learn that my outside-in view is wrong.

Product opportunity 01 · 10 → 100

The deployment process is a product problem in disguise

What looks like an ops bottleneck may actually be a missing product layer

When I looked at how Aurelian deploys today, my first reaction was the same: this is a strong team executing well under real constraints. Sub-six-week deployments in a government context are genuinely impressive. But speed alone is not the most important signal. The more important question is whether later deployments are becoming meaningfully easier, or whether too much of the same implementation burden still has to be recreated each time a new ECC goes live.

That distinction matters because not all deployment work is the same. Some of it is inherently high-touch and should probably remain so: cross-agency alignment, local validation, training, trust-building, and technical work that genuinely requires engineering involvement. But some of it appears different. It looks less like bespoke service work and more like repeated configuration work — the kind of work that often begins in deployment, but eventually belongs in a more repeatable product layer.

My hypothesis is that SOP setup may be one of those seams, at least in part. From the outside, a meaningful share of the variation seems to show up in call-type definitions, question sequences, routing logic, and local escalation paths. Those differences are real, and I would not assume they are trivial. But they may still be structurally similar enough that some portion can be represented through a configurable framework instead of being rebuilt from scratch for each deployment. The content varies. The shape often doesn’t.

Product observation

The key question is not whether every ECC can be standardized. It is whether a large enough middle of the deployment motion is repetitive, structured, and low-risk enough to become progressively more repeatable. If the answer is yes, the opportunity is significant. Not because deployment becomes fully self-serve, and not because engineering or onboarding disappears. It would not. The value is more specific: less handcrafted configuration per deployment, more reuse, and a motion that gets faster as it scales — not one that stays flat.

To be clear about what product can and cannot own here: model fine-tuning on local call history, CAD integration, and accent-specific transcription still require engineering involvement. But SOP configuration and redirect routing look much closer to true product surfaces. The most realistic first step is probably not a fully self-serve customer tool. It is an internal or guided configuration layer that helps Aurelian’s own team deploy faster and more consistently, and only later makes clear which parts, if any, should become admin-configurable for customers.

Product opportunity 02 · 100 → 1000

CAD integration: from recurring cost to one-time investment

Product can't negotiate the partnership — but it can build the foundation that makes it tractable

This one sits right at the boundary of what product owns. The strategic decision — whether to pursue certified partnerships with CAD vendors or broader channel distribution deals — belongs to the founders and BD. But the product still has a consequential role here, because the value of CAD coverage is not just whether Aurelian can connect to another platform. It is whether each new integration makes the next customer easier to win, easier to deploy, or both. That is the difference between integration as a recurring cost and integration as cumulative leverage.

That said, I would not flatten the technical reality here. CAD integration is still a real engineering problem: data contracts, workflow mapping, edge cases, latency, certification requirements, and vendor-specific constraints all matter. The point is not that integration complexity disappears. It is that its strategic value depends on whether the work gets absorbed into a cleaner, more reusable integration foundation over time, or whether each new customer still triggers too much bespoke effort.

From the outside, the technical integration problem is obviously real. Every time Aurelian encounters a CAD system it has not integrated before, there is meaningful engineering work to do. But the more important product question is whether that work is being turned into a reusable integration layer or merely repeated case by case. If the architecture is clean — with standardized data handoffs, clearer adapter boundaries, and less reinvention for each new platform — then the cost of supporting the next similar customer starts to fall. If not, CAD coverage expands capability, but not much compounding advantage.

Integrating CentralSquare and Hexagon alone could cover a very large share of the addressable market if those connections are deep enough and reusable enough to lower future deployment friction. That is the strategic upside. The moat is not simply “having integrations.” It is having an integration foundation that turns prior engineering work into lower marginal cost, better partnership credibility, and a cleaner story for the next similar customer. At the same time, I would not overstate product’s role here: in some cases, the real bottleneck may still be vendor access, certification, channel politics, or whether a CAD partner sees Aurelian as complementary rather than competitive. Product cannot solve that on its own. But it can ensure that when those doors do open, Aurelian is building on reusable ground rather than starting over each time.

Product observation

Product’s role here is not to create the partnership motion. It is to make each integration additive instead of bespoke. That means building an integration layer clean enough that certified partnerships become technically credible and commercially easier to support: clearer interfaces, more normalized patterns, better visibility into what is already supported, and less custom work when the next customer on a similar platform appears. Product cannot create the moat by itself, but it can determine whether integration work compounds into one.

Concretely, that could mean treating each new integration as a chance to improve the underlying layer rather than just close the immediate gap: clearer adapter boundaries, more standardized handoffs, better visibility into what is already supported, and less ambiguity around what is configurable versus what still requires net-new engineering. That is the kind of work that makes future partnerships easier to support and future deployments less custom, even when the commercial motion itself still sits with founders or BD.

If CAD vendors do not move quickly on certification, the fallback is still a BD motion — a channel or referral relationship that helps Aurelian get in front of ECC buyers without deep formal partnership. Product does not own that motion either. But it can support it by making the integration story legible: what is already supported, what is configurable, what still requires net-new engineering, and why the implementation burden should continue to fall over time.

Product opportunity 03 · 1000 → scale

The procurement problem: where product supports sales

The politics of government buying are not a product problem — but some of the proof required to move each buyer is

I want to be precise about what product can and cannot own here. Buying AI for a 911 center is not a single decision. It is a chain of decisions across multiple stakeholders — the ECC director, IT security, the budget committee, the dispatcher union, and county legal — each with different veto power, different risk thresholds, and different definitions of a “yes.” Navigating the politics of that process is still a CEO and sales problem. Product should not pretend otherwise.

But inside that process, there is a narrower and more leverageable product role. It is not to replace persuasion, relationship-building, or founder-led trust. It is to reduce how much of the factual proof required in each deal still has to be rebuilt from scratch. Some of what these buyers need is political. Some of it is relational. But some of it is concrete, repeated, and answerable: deployment outcomes, escalation visibility, auditability, security posture, operational ROI, and evidence that the system will improve — rather than destabilize — dispatcher workflows.

That is the seam I think product can own. Not the politics of the sale, but the repeatable proof layer underneath it. If those materials are assembled ad hoc in every deal, the sales motion remains highly founder-dependent. If they are built into standard, trustworthy, deployment-backed artifacts from the start — an ROI dashboard grounded in live results, a security and compliance packet, an escalation report, an audit trail, eventually even change-management evidence like dispatcher satisfaction or override rates — then the sales process becomes more legible and less dependent on re-explaining the same truths in every room.

Product observation

Product’s role here is narrow but high-leverage: build the outputs that convert each stakeholder’s recurring question into a more answerable one. Not because every objection can be solved with a document — it cannot — but because many of the most important ones require the same underlying evidence every time. The ROI model for the budget committee. The auditability and escalation logic for legal. The security posture for IT. Over time, even the union conversation may benefit from product-generated proof around workload shift, escalation quality, and dispatcher experience.

TThe key distinction is that these should not be treated as generic marketing collateral. They are strongest when they are product-derived, deployment-backed, and easy to trust because they are generated from the system itself rather than assembled as one-off sales slides. Product does not own the conversation. But it can make the conversation less fragile by ensuring that the proof layer is real, reusable, and available before the founders have to recreate it in every deal.

Section 04 · Judgment

If I were the founding PM: how I'd think about sequencing

Not a conclusion — a decision framework, and an honest invitation to push back

I want to end with something honest: sequencing these three opportunities is not a decision I can make credibly from the outside. Aurelian may ultimately need all three — a more repeatable deployment layer, a cleaner integration foundation, and a stronger product-derived proof layer for procurement. The real question is not which one sounds best in isolation. It is which one removes the next meaningful constraint on growth without creating more execution drag than the company can absorb right now.

That answer depends on a small set of variables that are only fully visible from the inside. If I were stepping into the first PM seat, I would not start by defending a favorite idea. I would start by figuring out which repeated burden is already concentrated enough, costly enough, and ready enough to become the next system the company builds.

Here's the framework I'd use to figure it out in the first 30 days:

Variable 01

Where is demand already concentrated?

If a large share of the next wave of ECC opportunities sits on platforms Aurelian already supports, then deployment repeatability likely has the fastest near-term payoff. If demand is clustering around one or two CAD ecosystems that are not yet covered, integration leverage may matter more. If deals are getting deep into review but repeatedly slowing in stakeholder sign-off, then the procurement proof layer may be the more urgent bottleneck. Pipeline concentration determines where product leverage will compound fastest.

Variable 02

What commitments are already tied to near-term revenue?

Founding-stage priorities are rarely chosen on a blank page. They are shaped by what current customers, pilots, and partners have already been promised — explicitly or implicitly. If near-term revenue depends on faster deployments, that changes the urgency of the configuration layer. If a specific CAD integration is the gating item for a meaningful deal, that changes the calculus. If the company is already doing founder-heavy work to push deals through procurement, the question is whether a stronger proof layer would reduce that burden soon enough to matter.

Variable 03

Which bet has the highest compounding value?

Not every painful problem is the right first system to build. The better question is which investment most reduces future marginal effort across sales, deployment, or expansion. A repeatable deployment layer can lower implementation burden across many customers. A reusable integration foundation can lower expansion friction across future platforms. A stronger proof layer can shorten or stabilize stakeholder review in future deals. The right first bet is not just the loudest pain point — it is the one most likely to keep paying back after the first use.

Variable 04

Is the organization ready to absorb that bet now?

Even the right leverage move can be mistimed. Readiness is not just engineering bandwidth. It is whether the company has the technical foundation, instrumentation, internal ownership, and operating room to turn one of these burdens into a real system without destabilizing the work already tied to current customers and current revenue. Sometimes the correct long-term move is obvious, but the right near-term sequencing is still constrained by what the organization can absorb.

What I'd do in 30 days

Sit in on active sales calls, deployment reviews, and customer check-ins to map where effort is still being recreated too often. I would want a clear view of pipeline concentration, repeated friction points, current customer and partner commitments, and the parts of the workflow that are most operationally expensive today.

What I'd do in 60 days

Translate those patterns into a sharper recommendation: which repeated burden is most worth turning into a system first, what should remain high-touch for now, and what enabling work has to happen before the first leverage bet is viable.

What I'd do in 90 days

Get the company aligned around one primary leverage bet, the success criteria behind it, and an execution path that improves scalability without disrupting the revenue-critical work already in motion. The goal would not be to make everything scalable at once. It would be to make the next scaling constraint explicit, owned, and meaningfully less manual.

The trade-off either way is the same: prioritizing one means something else waits, and whatever deal, customer, or internal burden sits behind what gets deprioritized becomes a cost the team is knowingly accepting. That cost should be explicit, tracked, and revisited — not forgotten because a decision was made.

These are my starting observations. I'd genuinely like to know where this matches how you're thinking about it — and where it doesn't. The places we disagree are probably the most interesting ones to talk through.
