I spent time researching Aurelian before writing this, and what stood out to me is that the product appears to have already cleared a difficult early threshold: it is not just technically interesting, but operationally credible. The combination of meaningful automation, relatively fast deployments, and live adoption across multiple states suggests the team has done something unusually hard for public safety software: built an AI product that can function in a high-stakes, low-trust environment and still get government customers live. That is not common, and it should not be underestimated.

From the outside, the question I kept coming back to is not whether the product works. It is whether the current product form is built for the next stage of scale. Proven viability is one thing; scalability is another. In many companies, that transition is where a founding PM can be especially useful: not by redefining the vision, but by helping the team decide what should remain high-touch, what should become repeatable, and where the next bottleneck is most likely to emerge.

The three observations below are my attempt to frame that transition through a product lens. They are not conclusions. They are hypotheses about where product may have the most leverage, where it should stay in its lane, and which scaling constraints seem most likely to matter next. They are also the places where I would be most interested to learn that my outside-in view is wrong.

Product opportunity 01 · 10 → 100

The deployment process is a product problem in disguise

What looks like an ops bottleneck is actually a missing product surface

When I looked at how Aurelian deploys today, my first reaction was: this is a strong team executing well under constraints. Sub-six-week deployments in a government context is genuinely impressive. But when I looked at what's inside those six weeks — the meetings, the manual SOP mapping, the hand-built logic trees — I saw something a PM should flag: work that belongs in the product, not in the deployment process.

Right now, every new ECC requires Aurelian's team to meet with law enforcement, fire, and EMS separately, map call types by hand, build redirect logic from scratch, and load local GIS data manually. It works. But it doesn't compound — the 50th customer takes the same effort as the 5th. That's the signal that something which should be a product feature is currently being handled by people.

The key insight is that the customization isn't actually that custom. Across every deployed ECC, variation follows a predictable pattern: different call type labels, different question sequences, different redirect targets — but the underlying structure is identical. Every call flow is a trigger, a set of questions, and an outcome. The content varies. The shape doesn't.

Product observation

The bottleneck isn't complexity — it's that Aurelian is manually doing work that customers could do themselves, if given the right tool. A self-serve Call Flow Builder turns a 4-week configuration sprint into a 2-hour onboarding session. This is the difference between a deployment motion that scales and one that doesn't.

To be clear about what product can and can't own here: model fine-tuning on local call history, CAD integration, and accent-specific transcription still require engineering involvement. But SOP configuration and redirect routing are product surfaces. The institutional knowledge that today lives in an Aurelian engineer's notebook can become a template library any ECC admin navigates on their own.

Product opportunity 02 · 100 → 1000

CAD integration: from recurring cost to one-time investment

Product can't negotiate the partnership — but it can build the foundation that makes it tractable

This one sits at the boundary of what product owns. The strategic decision — whether to pursue certified partnerships with CAD vendors or channel distribution deals — belongs to the founders and BD. But how Aurelian's integration layer is architected is a product decision, and it has direct consequences for how tractable those conversations become.

Right now, every time Aurelian encounters a CAD system it hasn't integrated before, an engineer builds the connection from scratch. There are approximately 5,748 PSAPs in the US, concentrated across four major vendors. Aurelian has integrated CentralSquare — confirmed in the Hamilton County deployment. But each new platform is a fresh engineering project. If the integration layer is built with clean interfaces and standardized data handoffs, the cost per new platform drops significantly, and the case for vendor partnership becomes much easier to make.

Integrating CentralSquare and Hexagon alone covers the majority of the addressable market. Every subsequent customer running either platform is a near-zero marginal integration cost. That's the moat — not the technology itself, but the compounding advantage of having done the integration work once, deeply, at the certification level.

Product observation

Product's role here is to build an integration layer clean enough that certified partnerships become technically low-friction. The BD conversation is the founders' to have — but if the engineering foundation is solid, that conversation starts from a much stronger position. Product can't create the moat, but it can build the ground the moat sits on.

If CAD vendors don't move fast on certification, the fallback is a channel deal — their sales teams recommend Aurelian to ECC customers in exchange for referral fees. That's a BD motion, not a product one. But product can support it by making the integration story easy to tell.

Product opportunity 03 · 1000 → scale

The procurement problem: where product supports sales

The politics of government buying aren't a product problem — but the artifacts that move each buyer are

I want to be precise about what product can and can't own here. Buying AI for a 911 center involves five different decision-makers — the ECC director, IT security, the budget committee, the dispatcher union, and county legal. Navigating the politics of that process is a CEO and sales problem. Product shouldn't pretend otherwise.

But inside that process, there's a specific thing product can build: the artifacts that give each buyer what they need to say yes. Today, these get assembled ad hoc for each deal. If product builds them as standard, ship-on-day-one materials — an ROI dashboard anchored to real deployment data, a security and compliance doc, an emergency escalation report — the sales motion runs without the founders in the room for every step.

Product observation

Product's role here is narrow but high-leverage: build the artifacts that convert each stakeholder's specific concern into a resolved question. The ROI model for the budget committee. The audit log and escalation report for legal. The dispatcher satisfaction data for the union. These aren't marketing materials — they're product outputs that happen to live in a sales context.

Section 04 · Judgment

If I were the first PM: how I'd think about sequencing

Not a conclusion — a decision framework, and an honest invitation to push back

I want to end with something honest: sequencing these three opportunities isn't a product decision I can make from the outside. It depends on variables only visible from inside — what the pipeline looks like, what commitments already exist with customers, whether engineering can run multiple workstreams in parallel. Both the Call Flow Builder and CAD integration need to happen. The question is whether they can run simultaneously, or whether one needs to go first — and that answer lives in the data I don't have yet.

Here's the framework I'd use to figure it out in the first 30 days:

Variable 01

What does the current pipeline look like?

If the next 20 inbound ECCs are on CentralSquare — already integrated — then Call Flow Builder has immediate ROI. If deals are stalling because of CAD incompatibility, that's a different answer entirely. Pipeline composition determines urgency.

Variable 02

What commitments already exist?

If a customer was promised a 6-week deployment and the team is already stretched, deployment efficiency is urgent now. If a large ECC said "we'll sign when you support Motorola CAD," that single conversation could unlock a deal — and it changes the calculus completely.

Variable 03

Can engineering and BD run in parallel?

CAD certification is a BD and engineering project. Call Flow Builder is a product and engineering project. If the same engineers own both, sequential is the only option. If BD can start vendor conversations while engineering ships the Builder, parallel is possible — and faster.

What I'd do in month one

Sit in on every active sales call, every deployment standup, and every customer check-in. Not to have opinions — to understand which of these three variables is the binding constraint right now. Then make the call. The sequencing decision is easy once you have the inputs. Getting the inputs is the real first job.

The trade-off either way is the same: prioritizing one means the other waits, and whatever customer or deal is blocked by what you deprioritized is a cost you're knowingly accepting. That cost should be explicit, tracked, and revisited — not forgotten because a decision was made.

These are my starting observations. I'd genuinely like to know where this matches how you're thinking about it — and where it doesn't. The places we disagree are probably the most interesting ones to talk through.

Three opportunities. Two product levers. One set of questions I'd want to answer before deciding. Happy to talk through any of this!
